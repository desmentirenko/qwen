Classic prisoner’s dilemma model
The classic prisoner’s dilemma predicts that it is rational for individuals, who are
charged with a crime and are given a choice, to confess and implicate others in order to get
lower sentences. For example, the prosecutors give each prisoner a choice of one-year
sentence if he confesses and implicates another and 25 year sentence if he does not. If one
prisoner confesses and implicates the other, and the other one does not confess, the person
who has voluntarily helped the prosecutors will get 1 year sentence, while the other person
will get 25 year prison term. If they both do not confess, the two men will get 3-year
sentences even though they are both innocent. (Table 1).

The prisoners’ dilemma model predicts that rational prisoners who are unbounded
by morality and friendship will both confess and get 10 year sentence even they
individually would be both better off not confessing. (Table 1). Assuming, as neoclassical
economics and rational choice theory in political science do, that prisoners maximize their
expected utility, confession is the optimal solution or the dominant strategy of the classic
prisoner dilemma.
The logic and the outcome of the prisoner’s dilemma game is essentially the same
for large number of prisoners, or “players,” and for other similar distribution of sentences,
or pay-offs. Other solutions, involving cooperation between prisoners can arise only if they
face repetitive choices, i.e. they presented with the dilemma multiple and indefinite
number of times, or if external payoffs affect stakes faced by players. Tit for tat strategy
beats other choices in nonfinite iterative prisoner’s dilemma games.1

Research question and previous studies
Many experimental tests of the prisoner’s dilemma found that cooperation is a
much more frequent outcome than that predicted by the model.2 However, these
experiments were often carried out among university students majoring in Economics.
They involved artificial situations and small stakes.3
The Great Terror in the Soviet Union presents an example of the prisoner's
dilemma situation.4 The question is whether political prisoners during the Great Terror
cooperated with prosecutors and voluntary confessed as the prisoner’s dilemma predicts.
The Great Terror in the Soviet Union came closest to the real-life prisoner’s
dilemma situation. It involved large numbers of individuals arrested on political charges
that carried capital punishment or sentences of varying length. Several million people were
arrested at the height of the terror in 1937-1938. In contrast to many previous studies
which denote the start of the Great Terror with waves of political arrests that followed the
assassination of Kirov in the end of 1934, this article regards political arrests in the
beginning of the 1930s as part of the Great Terror. This is in large part due to the focus of
the study on political prosecution of intellectuals in Ukraine which began in mass with the
Union of Liberation of Ukraine (SVU) show trial in 1930. Approximately 80 percent of
Ukrainian writers, 43 percent of legal scholars, 41 percent of ethnic and folk culture studies
scholars and anthropologists, 37 percent of historians, 36 percent of archeologists, 33
percent of geologists, 31 percent of specialists in study of literature and language, 27
percent of economists, 25 percent of physicists, 22 percent of librarians, 20 percent of
physicians, and 14 percent of mathematicians at the research centers of the All-Ukrainian
Academy of Sciences were arrested in the 1930s.

4
Soviet prosecutors demanded from the prisoners to confess to political crimes and
name associates in exchange for more lenient punishment. The confessions were used to
convict arrested individuals, as well as to arrest others. Confession was a necessary and
sufficient proof of guilt. Punishment for counterrevolutionary activity in the Soviet
criminal code ranged from execution to 3 years or more if there were softening
circumstances.5 Investigators promised less severe punishment to political prisoners who
cooperated with them and implicated others.
Political prisoners had to make their choices based on their expected sentences.
The absolute majority of political trials were conducted in secret and the NKVD
deliberately misinformed that prisoners who were executed were sentenced to 10 years.
Therefore, deception by the NKVD, which often gave more severe sentences than
promised, did not affect incentives of prisoners to confess.6
The totalitarian political system and the protracted sentences or executions meant
that possibilities of external enforcement and repeated interaction were minimal. Trials
were carried out without jury and witnesses by specially appointed courts and
commissions. They overwhelmingly resulted in automatic convictions that were rarely
overturned on appeal. For example, statistical data of the NKVD showed that about 200
thousands of arrested individuals in Soviet Ukraine were tried on political and spying
charges in 1937-1938. In 99.7 percent of cases they were found guilty and given sentences
ranging from the capital punishment (execution) to 3 years. Only 0.3 percent of cases
resulted in release.7
This study combines political science approaches and experimental economics
approaches and uses the quasi-experimental conditions of the Great Terror to evaluate the

5
prisoner’s dilemma model. An increasing number of socials scientists use history to test
rational choice models.8 However, many of these studies employ selective historical
evidence and often disregard historical facts that contradict rational choice theories.9 For
example, one study maintains that Stalin primarily relied on economic incentives and his
behavior was akin to behavior of kings and other authoritarian leaders and it was primarily
motivated by maximization of state revenues.10 This view disregards the role of communist
ideology in motivating the Soviet ruler, who imprisoned, exiled and killed millions of
people because they were regarded as class enemies. Similarly, it ignores the widespread
use of imprisonment and other forms of punishment for work-related violations.
Researchers found different patterns of behavior of political prisoners during the
Great Terror in the Soviet Union. For example, a previous study of the prisoner’s dilemma
model of the Great Terror concludes that most political prisoners confessed and implicated
others as the prisoner’s dilemma model predicts. Many historians emphasize mass
character of both voluntary denunciations and torture during the Great Terror but they do
not quantify the relative roles of these factors.11
These studies mostly rely on anecdotal accounts because archival materials on the
Great Terror were closed until Gorbachev’s policy of glasnost and the collapse of the
Soviet Union in the end of the 1980s and beginning of the 1990s. Recently declassified
documents from the Soviet archives show that one of the most cited publications on the
role of confessions during the Great Terror was written by a secret informer of the NKVD.
Because Konstantin Shteppa tried to conceal this fact he provided a distorted account of
his own behavior and behavior of other political prisoners, including those examined in

6
this study, and emphasized voluntary denunciations and voluntary cooperation of prisoners
with the NKVD.12
Methodology and data
This article analyzes behavior of more than 100 political prisoners arrested in the
Soviet Union in the 1930s. 13 These cases are linked to the political persecution of
Mykhailo Krawtchouk (Kravchuk), an internationally renowned Ukrainian mathematician,
who was arrested in 1938. Mykhailo Krawtchouk was an academician of the All-Ukrainian
Academy of Sciences (VUAN). He held mathematics chair at the National Technical
University of Ukraine, headed a mathematical statistics division in the Institute of
Mathematics of the VUAN, and taught at several universities in Kyiv. Krawtchouk’s
contributions to different areas of mathematics, mathematical physics and statistics are still
widely used.14
This article analyzes behavior of Mykhailo Krawtchouk, imprisoned individuals
mentioned in the materials of his prosecution and prisoners connected to his academic and
personal networks. They include people arrested on various political charges in the All-
Ukrainian Academy of Sciences (VUAN), the Institute of Mathematics of the VUAN, The
Institute of Physics of the VUAN, National Economic University in Kyiv, Ukrainian
Physico-Technological Institute (UFTI) in Kharkiv, and the Jet Propulsion Research
Institute (RNII) in Moscow, and in several other organizations. Since each of these
prisoners was prosecuted as a part of a larger case, behavior of all prisoners in these cases
is examined. The prosecution of more than 100 of individuals in these cases presents a
large N prisoner’s dilemma with several sub-dilemmas.

7
The Krawtchouk’s case is selected because it involves externalities (unintended
effects) pertaining to the invention and patenting of the electronic computer in the US and
rocket technology development in the Soviet Union. Krawtchouk’s mathematical work and
his political persecutions had an unintended effect on the invention and patenting of the
Atanasoff-Berry computer which was designed to speed up solution of mathematical
equations with help of methods developed by Mykhailo Krawtchouk. Krawtchouk’s
students and acquaintances included prosecuted scientists and engineers, such as Sergei
Korolev and Yuri Kondratyuk, who played significant roles in the masterminding the
Soviet space program and developing theory of space flight.15
The data for this study are obtained from recently declassified materials of
prosecution and rehabilitation of political prisoners in the former Soviet Union. This article
employs archival materials of interrogations and trials in the 1930s, legal reviews and
judicial rehabilitation of political prisoners in the 1950s-1990s, and secondary
publications.
The same criteria are applied to evaluate each prosecution case. Confessions are
classified as voluntary when prisoners during their first interrogations admitted their
alleged political crimes, implicated others and did not change their testimony during
subsequent interrogations, trial and in the aftermath of the trial. When archival-
investigative cases or subsequent judicial reviews of individual prosecutions contain both
confessions and references to use in these particular cases of interrogation methods, which
produced significant physical and psychological pain, such confessions are treated as
involuntary. When the testimony of prisoners radically changed after initial interrogations
or during their trial, such confessions are also regarded as involuntary because torture was

8
the most plausible reason for such sudden confessions. When prisoners consistently
refused to confess and implicate others, such behavior is classified as non confession.
The Krawtchouk case
Mykhailo Krawtchouk was arrested on February 21, 1938. He was charged with a
membership in an underground Ukrainian nationalist and terrorist organization and spying.
The accusation of a membership in such fictitious organizations was a typical charge
against intellectuals during the Great Terror. The allegations of stemmed from the fact that
Krawtchouk closely identified with Ukrainian culture, playing a major role in developing
Ukrainian mathematical terminology and mathematical education. Mykhailo Krawtchouk
published his works in French, German, Italian, and Russian, but he wrote most of his
books and articles in Ukrainian. It did not help that Krawtchouk had relatives and students
from a variety of ethnic and religious backgrounds: not only Ukrainian, but also German,
Jewish, Polish and Russian. He pointed out this fact to his interrogators but it had no effect
on their demands to confess in the political crimes.
The NKVD prosecutors wanted Mykhailo Krawtchouk to confess about his
subversive activity and to implicate others, e.g., his coworkers, students, and acquaintances
in the membership in the fictitious underground organization. Krawtchouk wrote in his
appeal to the Supreme Procurator of the USSR in 1940:
They demanded from me, first of all, to give a written confession of
being a member of this organization. They also demanded from me to
name the upper leadership of this organization and persons who
recruited me and whom I recruited, and who instructed me and whom
I instructed on the matters of the organization within the Academy of
Sciences. They demanded to describe the evolution of my counter-
revolutionary convictions, my counter-revolutionary work,
participation in the White Armies, my counter-revolutionary agitation
among students, creation of insurgent detachments by the members of
the organization, my spying activity, etc.16

9

Mykhailo Krawtchouk was charged with being “an active member and a leader” of
an organization of former Borotbisty. Borotbisty was a party established in 1918 by a left
wing of the Ukrainian Party of Socialist Revolutionaries, which advocated a socialist and
populist program, as well as the independence of Ukraine.17 Mykhailo Hrushevsky, the
first president of Ukraine during its brief independence in 1918 and the leading Ukrainian
historian, was politically close to the party of Ukrainian socialist revolutionaries. In
contrast to most socialist revolutionaries, Borotbisty leaders advocated cooperation with
the Bolsheviks. They joined the Bolshevik party in 1920 when it prohibited the existence
of all other political parties.
Mykhailo Krawtchouk was never a member of Borotbisty or any other party.
But evidence such as his official contacts with some of the former Borotbisty and
Hrushevsky’s history book that the NKVD confiscated during Krawtchouk’s arrest were
sufficient to link the Ukrainian mathematician to the prosecution of the fictitious
organization of former Borotbisty.
The materials of his prosecution show that Mykhailo Krawtchouk did not confess
and did not implicate other individuals voluntarily. Despite demands of a NKVD
interrogator, he refused to implicate former leaders of Borotbisty whom he knew in their
capacity as high-ranking government officials. The NKVD prosecutors tried to link
Krawtchouk to Andrij Khvylia, Panas Lubchenko and Volodymyr Zatonsky. These alleged
leaders of the underground organization were charged in 1937 after the NKVD obtained
incriminatory testimonies during interrogation of other political prisoners.18

10
Krawtchouk was charged with establishing connection with the counterrevolutionary
organization in 1933. At that time, Andrij Khvylia was deputy minister of education in
Soviet Ukraine. When he was arrested on August 13, 1937, Khvylia was the head of the
Directorate for the Arts. In his official positions and earlier as the head of the Communist
party propaganda department, Khvylia publicly accused many intellectuals of political
deviations. Most of these intellectuals were arrested on political charges. In one of the
most notable examples, Khvylia denounced Mykola Khvylovy, a Ukrainian writer, for
suggesting the slogan, “Away from Moscow.” Khvylovy committed suicide in 1933
because of the public campaign against him and arrests of other Ukrainian intellectuals. In
turn, Khvylia was denounced by some of his colleagues and arrested.19
During his interrogation, Andrij Khvylia confessed to being a leader, along with
more than dozen of other top government officials, of the underground organization. On
August 18, 1937, Khvylia wrote a declaration addressed to Stalin and other top party and
NKVD officials. The declaration presented cultural and academic contacts with Western
Ukrainian intellectuals in Poland as communication with foreign spies.20 However, during
his interrogation and confrontation with Panas Lubchenko in Moscow a few days later,
Khvylia renounced his confession. Khvylia told Stalin that he and other alleged members
of the organization were not guilty. Stalin, along with other members of the Soviet
Communist party leadership, was present during this interrogation and confrontation. Later
the same day, Khvylia changed his testimony and repeated his earlier confessions.
However, during his closed trial on February 5, 1938, he again renounced his confessions.
Such reversals indicated that the NKVD interrogators used torture to extract confessions
from Khvylia.21

11
Panas Lubchenko was another alleged leader of the organization to which
Krawtchouk was linked by the NKVD. In fact, Lubchenko was the head of the government
of Soviet Ukraine and a member of the Political Bureau, the governing body of the
Ukrainian branch of the Soviet Communist party. However, his membership in Borotbisty
party twenty years earlier as well as confessions obtained from political prisoners led to his
dismissal from the top party position. He faced an inevitable arrest. In his various prior
capacities, Lubchenko took part in denouncing many government officials and Ukrainian
intellectuals. For example, he served as the main public prosecutor during the 1930 show
trial of 45 intellectuals falsely accused in membership in SVU (Union for Liberation of
Ukraine). He did the same during a meeting of the Central Committee of the Ukrainian
branch of the Soviet Communist Party in the end of August 1937. This meeting reviewed
incriminating testimonies against Lubchenko that the NKVD had obtained from
interrogating prisoners such as Khvylia. Lubchenko denounced Khvylia and other
members of the alleged underground organization, but he denied his own involvement.22
Volodymyr Zatonsky was arrested on November 3, 1937. He was the Minister of
Education of Soviet Ukraine, a member of Communist party leadership, and an
Academician of the VUAN. He studied and then taught physics at Kyiv University around
the same time that Krawtchouk did in the 1910s. Zatonsky later became one of the
founders of the Ukrainian branch of the Bolshevik Party. Like many other party officials,
he was involved in political denunciations, and was denounced himself during the Great
Terror.23
During his interrogation by the NKVD, Zatonsky mentioned Krawtchouk’s name,
along with many other names, as a member of the underground organization. Materials of

12
his archival-investigative case, in particular, short length of time between his arrest and
detailed confession, as well as the large number of individuals named by Zatonsky,
indicate a voluntary nature of his confession.24
During Krawtchouk’s interrogation, NKVD prosecutors tried to present his
meetings with Zatonsky as contact between members of the underground organization. A
protocol of Krawtchouk’s interrogation stated that he informed Zatonsky about “his anti-
Soviet nationalist work at the Institute of Mathematics” and received new tasks. These
tasks included “promoting young nationalist cadres on the educational front,” “supporting
the old reactionary professorate,” and “directing it against young Soviet scientists.”25 In
fact, Mykhailo Krawtchouk visited Volodymyr Zatonsky several times in 1937 to counter
accusations that he was giving low grades to students at the Kyiv Polytechnic Institute
(now National Technical University of Ukraine). Some of his students and colleagues
denounced Krawtchouk’s teaching despite numerous testimonies to the contrary. Poor
quality of work was considered sabotage, a political crime, in Stalin’s Soviet Union.
Because Krawtchouk did not cooperate voluntarily with the prosecution and
refused to confess and implicate others, the NKVD interrogators switched their method to
torture. In his appeal letter, Krawtchouk summarized methods used by the NKVD
investigators in his case as follows:
I was devastated by these wild accusations, physically broken by the
night interrogations, in particular, by complete sleep deprivation for
11 days and nights, worsening of my heart disease, and means of
direct physical force. They were coercing me morally by screams and
moans of the people being tortured in the neighboring rooms.26


The NKVD frequently used such methods of interrogation to force political
prisoners to confess.27 In addition to beatings and psychological intimidation, Krawtchouk

13
described a torture method called “conveyer.” It involved constant interrogation of political
prisoners by NKVD interrogators and deliberate sleep deprivation that could last up to
several weeks. While prisoners were not allowed to rest or sleep, the interrogators rotated
daily.
By means of torture in prison and by threats to arrest his family, prosecutors
forced Krawtchouk to sign confession statements. Before his trial, new NKVD
interrogators threatened Krawtchouk not to retract his confession because he would remain
under control of the Soviet organs.28 However, Krawtchouk did retract his confession after
the trial ended.
The behavior of Mykhailo Krawtchouk during his interrogations contradicts the
prisoner’s dilemma model. He did not confess voluntarily in exchange for a more lenient
punishment. His sentence reaffirmed the high stakes involved in his choice not to confess
voluntarily. On September 23, 1938, in a trial lasting one half-hour, the Military Collegium
of the Supreme Court of the USSR sentenced Krawtchouk to 20 years in prison and 5 years
in exile. Mykhailo Krawtchouk was assigned to perform heavy manual work in a gold
mine in Kolyma region. Like a large proportion of political prisoners, Krawtchouk did not
survive the Kolyma camps. He died there in March 1942.
Mykhailo Krawtchouk was convicted for being a member of the Ukrainian
nationalist and terrorist organization, which “included” three of his former students.
Volodymyr Mozhar, a mathematics professor at the Kyiv Technologic Institute of Food
Production (now National University of Food Technologies), was one of them. He was
arrested on May 1, 1937, and charged with being an active member of the Ukrainian
nationalist terrorist organization.29 Mozhar was a researcher at the Division of

14
Mathematical Statistics at the Institute of Mathematics. Krawtchouk headed this division.
They also co-authored two books on mathematics.30
Mykhailo Krawtchouk refused to describe Mozhar as an “enemy of the people” in
an academic article he published in 1937.31 This fact was used to initiate a public campaign
of Krawtchouk’s denunciation that led to his dismissal from the academic positions and the
eventual arrest. Kommunist, the main official newspaper in Soviet Ukraine, published on
September 14, 1937, an editorial titled "Academician Krawtchouk Advertises Enemies."
The editorial was signed by Dmytro Grave, the director of the Institute of Mathematics of
the Ukrainian Academy of Sciences, and Kindrat Breus, the academic secretary of the
Institute. The article stated that in his piece in the journal Uspekhi Mathematicheskich
Nauk (Progress in Mathematical Sciences), Krawtchouk gave a favorable review of the
work of “enemies of the people” who were uncovered by the NKVD. The letter noted that
Mykhailo Krawtchouk had enough time to withdraw his article or to make “appropriate
corrections,” but he chose not to.
Volodymyr Mozhar did not confess during his interrogation in his membership in
the underground organization and he did not implicate Krawtchouk and others. His first
interrogation after being transported from Moscow, where he was arrested, to a prison in
Kyiv, is dated May 10, 1937. The protocol of his interrogation stated that Mozhar
categorically denied his membership in the organization. He was specifically asked by
Gaponov, a NKVD interrogator, to implicate Mykhailo Krawtchouk: “You are a student of
Krawtchouk, a known nationalist.” Mozhar confirmed that he was Krawtchouk’s student
but said that he did not know about the nationalist views of his teacher.32

15
Volodymyr Mozhar’s membership in 1919 in the Borotbisty party as well as books
found during his arrest served as evidence for nationalist charges. NKVD documents
characterized books confiscated during Mozhar’s arrest as “counterrevolutionary and
ideologically dangerous.” Almost all these books were published legally in the Soviet
Union by intellectuals who were arrested during the Great Terror. The NKVD also used
Mozhar’s earlier contacts with several anarchists and a confiscated book by Peter
Kropotkin, a famous 19th century Russian anarchist, to accuse him of membership in an
underground anarchist organization. It relied on testimony obtained from several other
individuals to corroborate these charges. During his interrogation on June 22, 1937, a
NKVD interrogator told Mozhar that the investigation had detailed evidence about his
“participation in a counterrevolutionary organization” and persons with whom he “jointly
conducted counterrevolutionary activity.” Mozhar again refused to confess in the
membership in the underground organization.33
Testimonies of three other imprisoned individuals and one witness provided the
NKVD with incriminatory evidence against Mozhar. Confessions by two of these political
prisoners were not voluntary. Circumstances of the confession of the third prisoner cannot
be established because archival materials and publications about Mozhar’s prosecution
include incomplete materials regarding this prisoner, who was arrested in Leningrad (now
St. Petersburg) in April 1937. 34
The confession of Israel Dubinsky, who was arrested on June 12, 1937, and whose
case had the same number as Mozhar’s case, was obtained by the NKVD by means of
torture. In 1956, Dubinsky testified to a KGB investigator who was reviewing his case that
all his confessions “furnished in 1937 were given as result of unbearable physical

16
coercion” and that investigators even were throwing a cat on his head and on his face.35 In
1939, Dubinsky lodged a complaint with the Prosecutor General of the USSR about his
conviction. He wrote that the NKVD interrogator distorted his testimony, in particular, by
presenting a gathering at Mozhar’s apartment as a meeting of counterrevolutionary
organization.36
The prosecution case of Mozhar and Dubinsky contains testimony of Bentsion
Aisenberg, who was arrested in April of 1937 on charges of membership in an anarchist-
Trotskyist terrorist organization. In his letter to the head of the NKVD in August 1937,
Aisenberg named Mozhar and Dubinsky as members of the same organization, but he also
stated that he abandoned his earlier refusal to confess.37 Such sudden change in behavior is
an indication that his confession was not voluntary.
Mozhar refused to confess during the interrogation and trial to all charges except
“anti-Soviet agitation” and was given a capital punishment. The refusal to confess was
contrary to his self-interest, as the prisoner’s dilemma shows. Mozhar paid the ultimate
price for non-cooperation with the NKVD, even though he had the chance to incriminate
Mykhailo Krawtchouk and others. Mozhar was executed immediately after the verdict was
announced on the 9th of November in 1937. As in many other cases, the verdict was not
made public. Only in the end of the 1980s, the family of Mozhar learned that he was
executed. Israel Dubinsky, who signed a confession under torture, was sentenced to 10
years.38 The behavior of both prisoners in this case contradicts the prisoner’s dilemma
model. Despite incentives to confess offered by the NKVD, neither prisoner chose to
cooperate with the prosecutors voluntarily and implicate others.

David Topolyansky, a mathematics professor and Krawtchouk’s colleague at Kyiv
University and the Division of Mathematical Statistics at the Institute of Mathematics, was
arrested on June 11, 1937. Krawtchouk and Topolyansky co-authored an article in a
mathematical journal. In another article published less than a year later, Krawtchouk
mentioned his joint work with Topolyansky at the Institute of Mathematics of the VUAN.
As in the case of Mozhar, this fact was held against Krawtchouk during the public
campaign against him which began in September 1937. During his interrogation and trial,
Krawtchouk did not implicate Topolyansky in membership in any fictitious underground
organizations.
Similarly, David Topolyansky refused to cooperate with the prosecutors and
implicate Mykhailo Krawtchouk and others. A summary statement of the prosecution in
October of 1938 concluded that “the investigation conducted in this case established that
Topolyansky, while being a member of anti-Soviet Trotskyist organization, was connected
with Sachnovsky and Krawtchouk (sentenced), members of this organization, and
conducted anti-Soviet activity.”39 However, the testimony about Krawtchouk came not
from Topolyansky, but from two witnesses. These witnesses, who were graduate students
at Kyiv University, informed the NKVD that Krawtchouk and Sachnovsky, the rector of
the university, defended Topolyansky against accusations of poor teaching quality and
helped to promote him to the position of a professor. 40 A summary statement of the
prosecution accused Topolyansky of using “bourgeois methods in teaching in order to
harm communist education of the youth.” According to the NKVD indictment,
Topolyansky said that only talented people could quickly learn mathematics.41

18
David Topolyansky was released in the end of December, 1938, after about a year
and a half in prison, due to the lack of evidence against him. His refusal to confess was a
major factor in his release. Another major factor, though, was that Krawtchouk and
Mozhar did not implicate him during their interrogations, even though it was in their self-
interests, as the prisoner’s dilemma model shows. The NKVD documents stated that
testimony obtained from Sachnovsky and used to arrest Topolyansky was not
incriminating enough to convict him. The testimony of Grygory Lozovyk, a professor of
ancient history at Kyiv University, against Topolyansky was discounted because it relied
on words of Sachnovsky.42 This was not for lack of effort by the NKVD. Sachnovsky and
Lozovyk, who were arrested as a part of the fictitious “Trotskyist Terrorist Organization”
in the beginning of 1936, were already sentenced and executed.43 Two witnesses in the
case did not provide enough evidence about “anti-Soviet activity” of Topolyansky.44
During his interrogation, Mykhailo Krawtchouk refused to present his academic
contacts with foreign mathematicians as spying and to implicate other political prisoners in
this fictitious crime, although the prisoners’ dilemma predicts that this went against his
self-interest. NKVD interrogators queried Mykhailo Krawtchouk about his foreign ties in
order to portray these contacts as spying activity. Krawtchouk described his contacts with
West Ukrainian, French, and German mathematicians and physicists, but he refused to
confess in spying.45 Krawtchouk stated that he suspended his foreign correspondence and
publication of his papers abroad in 1936 to avoid any repercussions. Before 1936,
Krawtchouk maintained academic and personal contacts with many noted foreign
mathematicians. They included Jacques Hadamard in France, David Hilbert and Richard
Courant in Germany, and Tullio Levi-Civita and Francesco Tricomi in Italy. Mykhailo

19
Krawtchouk received job offers from several leading US universities in the 1920s, but he
declined them.
The NKVD charged Krawtchouk with helping Mykola Tchaikovsky to spy for
Poland. Tchaikovsky, a Ukrainian mathematician, was born in the Galicia province, then
part of Austria, and was educated at the University of Vienna. After World War I, Galicia
became part of Poland and the Polish authorities restricted Ukrainian-language education
in universities and schools. Krawtchouk helped Tchaikovsky immigrate to Soviet Ukraine
and found him an academic position in Odessa University. The NKVD used this fact as
evidence for spying charges against Krawtchouk. Tchaikovsky was arrested in 1933, and
he was falsely charged with being a spy and a member of the underground Ukrainian
Military Organization (UVO). The NKVD interrogators presented the fact that he once
lived in Poland and visited France as evidence that he was a spy and asked him to choose
whether he wanted to confess of being a Polish spy or a French spy. Tchaikovsky did not
confess voluntarily during his interrogation.46
Although Mykhailo Krawtchouk was accused with ties to Borys Lagunov, he did
not provide any incriminating evidence against Lagunov. They became acquainted during
Lagunov’s brief work at the Institute of Mathematics in the beginning of the 1930s. Borys
Lagunov was arrested along with 11 other individuals in 1936. The NKVD charged him
with active participation in an underground organization “People Party” and with
preparation of terrorist attacks against Soviet party and government leaders. However,
Lagunov and other prisoners, with one exception, refused to confess voluntarily during
their interrogation, and they were tortured. He and other prisoners in his case were
sentenced to 3-5 years of imprisonment or exile. Borys Lagunov was arrested again on the

20
same charges in a GULAG camp in January 1938. However, materials about his second
arrest are insufficient to determine whether he confessed voluntarily or involuntarily.47
Twelve political prisoners implicated Krawtchouk in the membership in the
underground organizations and other political crimes, but they did this involuntarily, i.e. as
result of physical and psychological torture during their interrogations.48 These prisoners
included Mykhailo Ptukha, Director of the Institute of Demography of the VUAN,
Volodymyr Ivanushkin, Director of the Library of the VUAN, Fedir Polonsky, a researcher
at the Institute of Geology of the VUAN, Maxim Lukovsky, former secretary of the party
committee of the VUAN, Leonid Levytsky, Deputy Director of the Institute of
Experimental Biology of the VUAN, Josip Hermaize, history professor at Kyiv University,
Volodymyr Durdukovsky, director of a school in Kyiv, Grygori Kholodny, Director of the
Institute of Ukrainian Scientific Language of the VUAN, Grygori Ivanytsya, professor of
Ukrainian language at Kyiv University, Petro Suprunenko, Director of the Institute of
Transportation Mechanics of the VUAN, and Arnold Krister, professor of law at the
National Economic University in Kyiv.49
They all refused to confess voluntarily and were tortured. The physical and
psychological torture methods used by the NKVD in these and other similar cases included
sleep deprivation and protracted interrogations up to several weeks, beating, forced
isolation, food deprivation, forced standing in stress positions, threats of immediate
execution of the prisoner and his family and forced witnessing of torture of other prisoners.
Defection
Some political prisoners “defected” and voluntarily denounced Krawtchouk and
other individuals. For example, Stepan Feshchenko, a researcher at the Institute of

21
Mathematics and a Communist party candidate, who was arrested in December of 1937,
voluntarily denounced Mykhailo Krawtchouk during his interrogation. Feshchenko
disliked Krawtchouk, who thought him lacking in mathematical knowledge and ability. A
NKVD operative testified that Feshchenko wrote a letter to the NKVD in the beginning of
1936 informing about the “anti-Soviet feelings and nationalist views” of Academician
Krawtchouk.50
Stepan Feshchenko stated that Mozhar and Topolyansky were arrested because of
his denunciation to the NKVD. He also said that Mykhailo Orlov, the academic secretary
of the Institute of Mathematics and his friend, was arrested in 1936 because of his
denunciation. Feshchenko declared that he wanted to denounce others. He was released on
February 22, 1938, as a reward for his denunciations.51
Mykhailo Orlov was charged in the membership in the Trotskyist Terrorist
Organization. In addition to his position at the Institute of Mathematics, Orlov was a high-
ranking Communist party official. Archival materials of his prosecution indicate that he
confessed voluntarily. Three days after his arrest, Orlov admitted his membership in this
fictitious organization and implicated others.52
As a seksot (secret informer) of the NKVD since 1935, Serhii Tutkovsky
(Lvovych) provided incrimination and false information that led to arrests and convictions
of Volodymyr Mozhar, Borys Lagunov and many other people connected to Mykhailo
Krawtchouk’s case. Tutkovsky was a former Borotbist. He joined the Bolshevik party and
taught philosophy at Kyiv University. Tutkovsky confessed voluntarily soon after his
arrest in September 1937 in a membership in the fictitious Trotskyist Terrorist
Organization, which he helped to create as a secret informer of the NKVD.53

22
Konstantyn Shteppa, professor of ancient history at Kyiv University, was also
involved in Krawtchouk’s case and voluntarily cooperated with the NKVD. He was
arrested in March of 1938 and charged with participation in an underground Ukrainian
nationalist organization and spying for Japan. However, archival documents and research
by historians show that Konstantyn Shteppa was a seksot who denounced people to the
NKVD.54 At the special session of the military tribunal on April 16, 1939, Shteppa stated
that the NKVD gave him especially important tasks in uncovering Ukrainian nationalist
organizations and that he provided much material to accomplish these tasks. He also
clamed credit for uncovering “the nationalist group” headed by Sachnovsky, Lozovyk, and
others at Kyiv University.55
Reports of secret informers to the NKVD in Krawtchouk’s case are not yet opened
for researchers. However, indirect evidence points towards Konstantyn Shteppa as one of
the secret informers in this and other related cases. In the mid 1930s, Shteppa established
ties with Krawtchouk, even though they worked in very different academic fields. As
Shteppa himself acknowledged during the closed session of the trial, the NKVD officials
installed secret informers close to people they considered politically suspicious.56
Shteppa’s behavior during the Great Terror fits the prisoner’s dilemma model. He is
an example of a rational self-interested individual, unconstrained by any ethical values.
Throughout his life, Konstantyn Shteppa constantly changed his behavior and political
stance according to changes in incentives. He fought the Bolsheviks during the Civil War,
but turned into “a non-party Bolshevik” after they won the war. Archival documents
recently uncovered by historians show that during the Nazi occupation of Ukraine, Shteppa
became a German secret police informer. He became the chief editor of the main pro-

23
German newspaper in Kyiv in the end of 1941, after the Nazis executed the paper’s
previous editors for their association with the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists. He
published many pro-Nazi, anti-Soviet, anti-Semitic and anti-American articles, and earned
praise for his active collaboration from the commander-in-chief of the German Army
stationed in Ukraine.57 However, after the war ended with the Nazi defeat, Shteppa again
conveniently changed his behavior and public views. For instance, he received an
immigration sponsorship and material assistance from Isaac Don Levine, a Jewish
immigrant from the Russian Empire and a prominent American journalist. Similarly,
Shteppa used support from Niels Bohr and Alexander Dallin, Jewish scholars, in
publishing his books.58
In his books, Konstantyn Shteppa portrayed himself as a victim of the Great
Terror. He described several secret informers but failed to acknowledge his service to the
NKVD.59 Contrary to his claims in these publications, Shteppa provided distorted accounts
of his own behavior and behavior of other people involved in his case. For example,
Konstantyn Shteppa referred in his publications to Georgy Lozovyk, a history professor at
Kyiv University, as his close friend.60 But, as noted, Shteppa voluntarily stated during his
trial that he denounced Lozovyk to the NKVD. Georgy Lozovyk was arrested as a former
Menshevik. After he left the Soviet Union, Konstantyn Shteppa established close ties with
Borys Nikolaevsky, one of the leaders of émigré Mensheviks in the US. Shteppa received
assistance from Nikolaevsky in obtaining a Ford Foundation grant for his book on Soviet
historians.61
The Union of Liberation of Ukraine (SVU) Case

24
Krawtchouk’s name was mentioned in several testimonies obtained by the NKVD
prosecutors from the accused in a big show trial held in 1930. Forty five intellectuals were
accused in forming an underground organization, the Union of Liberation of Ukraine
(SVU). The fictitious accusations and the show trial were masterminded by the Communist
party leaders and the GPU, as the NKVD was then called. Stalin himself sent a secret letter
to Kossior and Chubar, Communist Party leaders of Ukraine, with instructions on how to
stage the show trial and add new charges of poisoning communist leadership. Mykhailo
Krawtchouk was appointed as a public prosecutor at the SVU show trial but he declined to
participate despite significant risks that this decision entailed. During the public campaign
against Krawtchouk in 1937, this fact was used as an evidence of his anti-Soviet activity.
Materials of Krawtchouk’s prosecution state that Josip Hermaize recruited him in
1928 into a “counter-revolutionary and terrorist organization.” Josip Hermaize, a Jewish
professor of Ukrainian history at Kyiv University and head of the Arheographic
commission of the VUAN, was convicted as one of the leaders of the SVU in 1930.
Krawtchouk and Hermaize studied together at Kyiv University in the 1910s. They taught at
the First Ukrainian Gymnasium in 1919-1920 and then at Kyiv University.
During his interrogation at the SVU case, Josip Hermaize like the majority of other
prisoners, refused to give voluntary confession for several months. In 1989, a review of the
materials of the SVU case by a special commission concluded that 42 out of 45 accused
did not confess voluntarily during their interrogations.62 However, Hermaize and most of
the others were forced by the NKVD to change their testimony and implicate each other
and Mykhailo Krawtchouk.63

25
Interrogation of Rocket Scientists
Serhii Korolev, a former Krawtchouk's student at the Kyiv Polytechnic Institute,
was arrested in Moscow in June 1938. Mykhailo Krawtchouk helped Korolev to get
admission to the Kyiv Polytechnic Institute by waiving a two-year work experience
requirement introduced for people of non-proletarian origin.64 Korolev later transferred to a
Moscow Institute and joined in the 1930s a rocket program headed by Tukhachevsky, a
Red Army Marshall.
Serhii Korolev was arrested after his imprisoned colleagues at the rocketry institute
provided testimony which implicated him. However, this testimony was not voluntary.65
Korolev refused to confess voluntarily and was tortured. He confessed but later renounced
his testimony. Korolev was implicated by testimonies of Langemak and Kleimenov, two
leaders of the Jet Propulsion Institute (RNII).66 Their testimony was obtained by means of
torture. Georgy Langemak and Ivan Kleimenov were arrested on November 2, 1937
following arrest of Tukhachevsky and several other top Soviet military officials who were
falsely accused of being German agents.67 Valentyn Glushko, arrested in March 1938, also
confessed of being a member of the anti-Soviet organization and a German spy. However,
he renounced his testimony in letters to Stalin and Andrij Vyshinsky, the prosecutor-
general, and stated that he was tortured during his interrogation.68
The behavior of these four prisoners contradicts the prisoner’s dilemma model.
During their interrogations and trials they did not confess voluntarily in exchange for a
more lenient punishment. Langemak and Kleimenov were executed. Korolev received 10
year sentence, while Glushko was given 8 years. The difference in length of sentences
reflected primarily differences in their alleged roles in underground organizations.

26
This case had externalities pertaining to the Soviet space program. In the 1950s-
and 1960s, Serhii Korolev was Chief Designer of the Soviet space program, and he
masterminded launches of the first artificial satellite (Sputnik), the first man and first
woman in space, and the first spacecrafts to reach the Moon and Venus and fly by Mars.69
Korolev’s belief that he was prosecuted because of Glushko’s testimony led to his tensions
with Glushko who became a leading rocket engine designer. This had a negative effect on
the Soviet manned flight program to the Moon in the 1960s, and is considered one of
reasons for the failure of this program. Korolev chose a much less experienced jet engine
designer instead of Glushko to develop rocket engines for a large rocket which was
supposed to fly Soviet cosmonauts to the Moon. All launches of this rocket ended in
failure because of problems with engines. Unexpected death of Korolev during a surgery in
1966, which was in part attributed to a broken skull during his interrogation by the NKVD,
also negatively affected the Soviet space efforts, including the secret program of the
manned flight to the Moon.70
NKVD interrogators questioned Mykhailo Krawtchouk about his acquaintances
from the Volyn region, in particular about family of Kondratyuks. Oleksander Shargei,
who is now regarded as one of the founders of the theory of space flight, adopted
documents and identity of Yuri Kondratyuk, a member of this family. Shargei did this to
hide his involuntary service in the White Army from the Soviet authorities. Such service
and adoption of fake identity documents were regarded as a political crime and carried a
capital punishment in the Soviet Union.71
Mykhailo Krawtchouk was probably the only person who personally knew both
real Yuri Kondratyuk and Oleksander Shargei. Shargei used identity of Yuri Kondratyuk

27
when he visited Krawtchouk in 1933 for consultations concerning mathematical
calculations for a design of a large wind-powered electricity station in Crimea. This station
was not built because of political arrests in a Soviet ministry which funded this project.
However, a number of Shargei’s ideas were later adapted by his collaborator in designing
Ostankino TV tower in Moscow. Erected in 1967, this was the tallest building in the
world.72
Mykhailo Krawtchouk did not reveal anything that might have led to the
uncovering of this illegal identity change by Oleksander Shargei. He also did not implicate
Volodymyr Kondratyuk, Yuri Kondratyuk’s brother. Volodymyr Kondratyuk provided
Shargei with the documents of his brother who died in 1921. If Krawtchouk were to
implicate Volodymyr Kondratyuk or Oleksander Shargei, there was a significant
possibility that their secret would have been uncovered by the NKVD. However,
Krawtchouk minimized his contacts with Kondratyuks during his interrogation and stated
that they were people of Russian culture, even though Volodymyr Kondratyuk was an
ethnic Ukrainian and worked as a teacher of Ukrainian literature in the 1920s.73
Similarly, Volodymyr Kondratyuk did not confess during his interrogations
about the fact that he provided Shargei with identity documents of his brother. Volodymyr
Kondratyuk was first arrested in 1930 along with four other teachers of a high school in
Kyiv. He and other teachers were accused in membership in a fictitious
counterrevolutionary organization. Volodymyr Kondratyuk did not implicate his fellow
teachers and Mykhailo Krawtchouk. Because the NKVD prosecutors could not obtain
confessions from the arrested individuals, Volodymyr Kondratyuk was released after
spending 6 months in prison.74 In 1932, he was arrested again on similar charges.

28
However, Volodymyr Kondratyuk again did not confess. He was released after three
months of interrogation.75
Oleksander Shargei, who lived under name of Yuri Kondratyuk, was arrested in
Siberia in 1930 on charges of “sabotage” along with several other engineers. Materials of
his prosecution show that he did not confess and implicate others during his interrogation.
The uncovering of real identity of Shargei and likely disappearance of his works from the
public domain could have affected not only his fate but also the competition between the
Soviet and US space programs, in particular, their efforts in the manned flight to the Moon.
Shargei-Kondratyuk was the first to suggest a lunar orbiter rendezvous (LOR) method of
flying to the Moon. He proposed many other fundamental solutions, which were later used
by the Soviet and American space programs, such as use of atmosphere to slow down
descent of spacecraft.
The LOR method, employed by the NASA during the Apollo program, had
significant technological and economic advantages over the earth-orbit rendezvous method
suggested by Von Braun and the direct descent method which was also considered by the
NASA in the early 1960s. The LOR is credited by experts with enabling the US to land on
the Moon by the end of the 1960s, a key political objective that president Kennedy
declared in 1961 to counter the Soviet lead in space. This method also saved several
billions of dollars compared to other methods considered by the Apollo Program.76
The arrest of Shargei-Kondratyuk in the 1920s and his disappearance during
World War II made not only him but also his ideas, like the LOR, politically dangerous in
the Soviet Union. After Nazi Germany attacked the Soviet Union, Kondratyuk became a
soldier in the Red Army. He was either killed in action or was captured and killed by the

29
Nazis along several million other Soviet POWs.77 Captivity was considered as treason by
the Soviet government. Discovery of Kondratyuk’s work in German territory captured by
the Soviet troops in the end of World War II created suspicions that he might have worked
for the Nazi missile program. Soviet rocket scientists initially preferred the Earth-orbit
rendezvous method for their manned Moon program, and this depraved them of a key
advantage compared to the US Apollo program. They switched to the LOR concept only in
1964, more than two years after the Apollo program did.78
The UFTI case
In the 1930s mathematicians and physicists belonged to the same division of the
VUAN, worked in the same university departments, and their research often intersected.
Krawtchouk’s works in mathematical physics spanned both disciplines. Contrary to the
prisoner’s dilemma model, Mykhailo Krawtchouk did not implicate voluntarily any
physicists including Oleksander Goldman. Goldman, director of the Institute of Physics of
the VUAN in Kyiv, chaired the special commission of the Academy that was set-up
following the publication of the op-ed denouncing Mykhailo Krawtchouk in September
1937 and investigated allegations against him in assisting “enemies of people” and spies.
The commission concluded that these charges were valid and recommended to dismiss
Krawtchouk from his position as the head of a division at the Institute of Mathematics.
Oleksander Goldman was himself arrested in January 1938 on charges of spying
and membership in a Ukrainian nationalist organization. These fictitious charges were
based on testimonies of other prisoners, reports by secret informers, and intercepted
foreign correspondence. In contrast to his actions during the work of the special

30
commission, Goldman during his interrogation and trial did not confess and did not
implicate Krawtchouk and others.79
Sixteen people, including the noted physicists, such as Lev Landau, a future Nobel
Prize winner, were arrested in the case of the Ukrainian Physico-Technological Institute
(UFTI). They were charged with counterrevolutionary activity, spying for Germany,
Poland and Great Britain, and sabotage. These charges stemmed from the fact that several
refugee physicists from Germany and Austria worked at the Institute and that many Soviet
physicists visited the Rutherford laboratory at Cambridge and maintained contacts with
their foreign colleagues.
The UFTI was headed by Oleksander Leipunsky. Like Krawtchouk and Goldman,
Leipunsky was one of a handful physicists and mathematicians elected as Academicians of
the VUAN. Leipunsky was arrested on June 14th 1938 and charged with being a German
and British agent. On June 24th he signed a confession which implicated Fritz Houtermans,
Oleksander Weissberg, Lev Landau, and Lev Shubnikov in spying, but he only admitted
helping these “enemies of the people.” Leipunsky was released on August 9, 1938
ostensibly because of the lack of evidence even though several other prisoners implicated
him under torture.80
Lev Shubnikov, head of the laboratory of low temperatures at the UFTI, confessed
in spying and other counterrevolutionary activity the day after his arrest on August 6,
1937. He implicated many other physicists from the UFTI. Shubnikov hopped that his
voluntary confession would reduce his punishment and avert arrest his pregnant wife.
However, he was executed. Only in the beginning of the 1990s the fact of his execution
was revealed to his wife and to the public.81

31
The materials of their prosecution show that three physicists arrested in the UFTI
case confessed voluntarily, and 10 did not confess voluntarily. Six of the prisoners, who
refused to confess voluntarily, pleaded guilty because of physical and psychological
torture. Three of the prisoners did not confess even under torture.82
Conclusion
The prisoner’ dilemma model applied to the Great Terror in the Soviet Union
implies that political prisoners had an incentive to confess and implicate other prisoners.
Since the other prisoners had similar incentives, they also confessed. As a result, all of the
prisoners ended up worse off than if they all refused to confess.
The analysis of Krawtchouk’s case and related cases, such as the Union of
Liberation of Ukraine (SVU), the Jet Propulsion Institute, and UFTI cases, finds weak
support for the prisoner’s dilemma model. Among 177 cases of individuals arrested on
political charges in these cases, 13 percent gave voluntarily confessions and implicated
other prisoners. The absolute majority of the prisoners who provided voluntary confessions
and implicated others were Communist party members or secret informers of the NKVD.
When this factor is held constant, the rates of voluntary confessions among Ukrainians,
Jews and Russians do not differ significantly.
