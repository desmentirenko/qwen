Abstract

This study examines democratic orientations and their determinants in post-Orange Ukraine from
the perspective of a political culture theory. The research question is, to what extent is political
culture in Ukraine supportive of democracy. This paper uses data from different waves of the
Pew Global Attitudes surveys, the World Values Surveys, and the International Foundation for
Electoral Systems surveys to examine public views towards democracy in Ukraine. The paper
compares democratic values in Ukraine with those in Poland and Russia, two similar post-
communist countries with differing political cultures and divergent trajectories of
democratization. It also examines changes in the levels of pro-democratic attitudes in post-
Orange Ukraine compared to the Orange and pre-Orange periods. Finally, this study implements
multiple regression analysis of the determinants of democratic attitudes. The analysis shows
limited effects of historical democratic legacies and Catholicism on pro-democracy views. With
some exceptions, adherents of major parties do not differ significantly in terms of their degree of
support for democracy. This study suggests that partially democratic and partially authoritarian
political culture impedes the future prospects for consolidated democracy in Ukraine.

Democracy and Political Values in Ukraine
This paper examines the prospects for democracy in post-Orange Ukraine from a
political culture perspective. Ukraine is a good case for analyzing democratic and undemocratic
political values or culture. As a result of the “Orange Revolution” in 2004, when mass public
protests prompted a reversal of the fraudulent presidential elections, it became the most
democratic country in the post-Soviet region, with the exception of the Baltic states. For
example, the Freedom House democracy index classified Ukraine as the only “free” post-Soviet
country, excluding the Baltic states, every year from 2005 to 2009 (Freedom House, 2012). In
this regard, Ukraine was considered variously as either a democratic outlier or a potential model
to follow for semi-democratic or authoritarian post-Soviet states, such as Russia and Belarus.
(See, for example, Myagkov, Ordeshook, and Shakin, 2009; Levitsky and Way, 2010).
However, the victory of Viktor Yanukovych in the 2010 presidential elections brought a
reversal in the trajectory of democratization. For instance, since 2010 the Freedom House has
classified Ukraine as “partly free,” putting this post-Soviet country into the same category it was
in prior to the “Orange Revolution” (Freedom House, 2012). Although Yanukovych won
democratic elections in 2010, he was involved in an attempt to gain power by falsifying the
results of the 2004 presidential elections in his favor (See Lukinova, Myagkov, and Ordeshook,
2011; Myagkov, Ordeshook, and Shakin, 2009; Wilson, 2005). After the 2010 presidential
elections, the Party of Regions established a coalition by employing quasi-legal means to change
the parliamentary majority. The ruling coalition includes the Communist Party of Ukraine, a
successor to the Ukrainian branch of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union, which was a
totalitarian organization for most of its history. The Party of Regions won local elections in most
Eastern, Southern, and Central regions in 2010, but these elections were less free and fair

compared to previous elections. A number of recent studies argue that post-Orange Ukraine
under Yanukovych is in the process of a retreat from democracy towards an autocracy, and that
political values, particularly in primarily Russian-speaking Eastern and Southern regions that
overwhelmingly voted for Viktor Yanukovych, are authoritarian (See, for example, Kramer,
Nurick, Wilson, Alterman, 2011; Kuzio, 2011; Menon and Motyl, 2011).
In addition to the victories of Viktor Yanukovych in the 2010 presidential contest and
his Party of Regions in the local elections, strong votes for Svoboda in many Western Ukrainian
regions in the 2010 local elections also raise questions regarding the future of a democracy and
the strength of democratic values in Ukraine. Svoboda (Freedom), a radical nationalist party,
which in addition to electoral politics advocated the use of non-democratic methods, became the
dominant party in the regional councils in Galicia and one of the major oppositional parties on
the national level. This party presents itself as an ideological successor to the Organization of
Ukrainian Nationalists, which advanced a semi-totalitarian political program combining fascism
and radical nationalism and employed terrorism for most of its existence in Ukraine during the
1930s and the 1940s. Svoboda has a significant chance to enter the national parliament in the
2012 parliamentary elections by clearing the 5% vote threshold on the party list ballot and/or
winning seats in single-mandate districts, primarily in Galicia. A very limited number of
previous studies has analyzed the rise of this radical nationalist party and its implications for the
future of democracy in Ukraine (see, for instance, Shekhovtsov, 2011).
The research question is concerned with the extent to which political culture or values
in Ukraine are supportive of a democratic political system. This study uses comparative and
statistical analyses of several major surveys, such as the World Values Surveys, Pew Research

5

Center’s Global Attitudes surveys, and the International Foundation for Electoral Systems (IFES)
surveys to examine fundamental orientations towards major aspects of democracy in Ukraine.
Numerous previous studies have pointed out such factors as the level of economic
development, civil society, political institutions (such as presidential or parliamentary systems),
and international linkages and environments (such as integration with the European Union), that
promote or hinder democracy in various countries, including Ukraine (see, for instance, Lai and
Melkonian-Hoover, 2005; Przeworski, Alvarez, Cheibub, and Limongi, 2000; Levitsky and Way,
2010; Linz, 1990; Lipset, 1959; Lipset and Lakin, 2004). However, democracy also requires a
supportive political culture or values, which involve fundamental orientations, beliefs, and norms
(see, for example, Almond and Verba, 1965; Inglehart and Welzel, 2003; Lipset, 1994; Lipset
and Lakin, 2004; Putnam, Leonardi, and Nanetti, 1993).
A number of major survey-based analyses have concluded that mass political values in
pre-Orange Ukraine were generally supportive of democracy, and that they did not preclude
emergence of a consolidated democracy (Gibson, 1996; Haerpfer, 2008; Reisinger, Miller, Hesli
and Maher, 1994). However, a period of democratization soon after Ukraine’s independence was
followed by the emergence of a semi-authoritarian regime during the presidency of Leonid
Kuchma. Previous studies generally presented the “Orange Revolution” in 2004 as a democratic
breakthrough or a democratic revolution that turned Ukraine from a semi-autocracy into a
democracy. Indeed, democracy was often regarded as the most likely future path for this post-
Soviet state (see Aslund and McFaul, 2006; D’Anieri, 2005; Hale, 2006; Katchanovski, 2008;
Kuzio, 2010; Motyl, 2008; Stepan, 2005; Way, 2005; Wilson, 2005).
Mass protest actions against falsification of the results of the 2004 presidential
elections and the victory of Viktor Yushchenko in the repeat second round of the 2004 elections

6

were often cited as evidence of democratic values being held by Ukrainians. Particularly Western
Ukraine, whose residents overwhelmingly voted for Yushchenko and actively participated in
mass protest actions, was frequently regarded as a bastion of democracy. Some studies argued
that a younger generation socialized in an independent Ukraine and whose members
disproportionally participated in the “Orange Revolution” became the bearer of democratic
political values. Stronger democratic values in Ukraine compared to Russia were a key factor
explaining their diverging trajectories in terms of democratization (see Brudny and Finkel, 2011;
Kuzio, 2010; Motyl, 2008; Nikolayenko, 2007).
However, some scholars argued that the “Orange Revolution” was not a revolution at
all, but rather an evolutionary development that did not bring about fundamental changes in
political values, formal and informal political institutions, and types of parties and leaders.
Ukraine’s foreseeable future was more compatible with a semi-democratic political system than
with a Western-type liberal democracy. Many studies that predicted relatively favorable
prospects for democracy in Ukraine after the “Orange Revolution” ignored strong currents of
undemocratic values that were shared to a certain extent even by political leaders and parties that
branded themselves as democratic and pro-Western, such as Victor Yushchenko and his “Our
Ukraine” bloc, and Yulia Tymoshenko and her Yulia Tymoshenko Bloc (BYuT). This was also
the case with similar predictions concerning prospects for democracy in other post-Soviet
countries, such as Georgia and Kyrgyzstan after the “color revolutions” in the mid-2000s and
Russia under Boris Yeltsin in the 1990s (see Katchanovski, 1995; Tudoroiu, 2007).
Political values in Ukraine are expected to be less like those of the mostly democratic
political cultures in post-communist countries such as Poland and more akin to semi-democratic
or authoritarian values among the general public, political elites, and parties in such post-Soviet

7

countries as Russia or Georgia. Such hybrid semi-democratic/semi-authoritarian regimes
combine multi-party systems and partly competitive elections with an authoritarian-leaning
leader and a party of power. To maintain power, these leaders, such as Mikheil Saakashvili in
Georgia and Vladimir Putin in Russia, and their parties rely not only on their popularity among
the voters but also on administrative resources, including, for instance, government control over
considerable parts of mass media.
Democratic, semi-democratic, or authoritarian political cultures are often linked to
historical and religious legacies. For instance, the legacy of British colonial rule has had a
positive impact on the levels of democracy present in various countries of the world (Lipset and
Lakin, 2004). Similar lingering effects of legacies are likely to be found in Ukrainian regions that
experienced various political systems prior to World War One and World War Two. These
historical legacies include the relatively more democratic Austro-Hungarian rule before World
War One in Galicia, Bukovyna, and Transcarpathia, as compared to the legacies of authoritarian
rule present in the Russian Empire and the Ottoman Empire in other Ukrainian regions, and the
relatively more democratic Czechoslovak, Polish, and Romanian rule in Western Ukraine
(specifically Galicia, Bukovyna, Transcarpathia, and Volhynia) compared to totalitarian rule in
Soviet Ukraine between World Wars One and Two (see for example, Birch, 2000; Katchanovski,
2006). However, the effect of these historical legacies on democratic culture in the various
regions of Ukraine is likely to prove less significant than the effect on pro-nationalist/pro-
Western vs. pro-Communist/pro-Russian values, considering that the democratic experience
resulting from the Austro-Hungarian Empire and inter-war-Poland, Romania, and
Czechoslovakia was generally relatively short in duration, and because participation of Ukraine

8

in elections and democratic parties was much more limited compared to the participation of
Poles or Czechs.
Compared to Muslim countries, both Protestant and (since the last decades of the 20th
century) Catholic countries have a much greater likelihood of being democratic (see Huntington
1991). Even the series of overthrows of longstanding authoritarian governments in 2011 in such
Muslim countries as Egypt, Tunisia, and Libya, and the mass protest movements or violent
rebellions against authoritarian regimes in several other countries, such as Yemen and Syria, did
not alter this pattern. Moreover, prospects for consolidated liberal democracy in states such as
Egypt, Tunisia, and Libya remain relatively low, as evidenced by strong popular backing for
radical Islamist parties. Even the overthrow of dictatorships in Afghanistan and Iraq by military
force and the relatively prolonged occupations of these countries by the United States with the
support of some other major Western democracies, such as the United Kingdom, did not turn
these countries into self-sustaining democracies, despite predictions to the contrary by many
scholars and policy-makers in the West.
Western Christian religious legacies, compared to Muslim and Orthodox Christian
legacies, tend to have positive effects on the democratization of post-communist states (Pop-
Eleches, 2007). A similar effect of religious legacies can be expected in Ukraine, since the
country has a significant Greek Catholic population, concentrated mostly in Galicia and
Transcarpathia, while its other regions are predominantly Orthodox. However, this effect might
be muted to a certain extent, because Greek Catholicism combines allegiance to the Vatican and
the Pope with the rituals and traditions of Orthodox Christianity and because the Greek Catholic
Church was banned in Soviet Ukraine from the end of the 1940s until the end of the 1980s.

9

In addition to the above-mentioned reasons, the effects of historical and religious
legacies are diminished on a national level because the relatively more democratic historical
legacies are generally limited to regions of Western Ukraine, whose population constitutes only
about one-fifth of the total population of Ukraine. The Greek Catholic legacy is even more
restricted according to region in Ukraine. But the impact of these elements of political culture is
expected to be noticeable in comparisons of the levels of democratic attitudes in Ukraine with
those of Russia and Poland. While tilting more towards Russia than Poland in terms of pro-
democratic orientations, the pro-democratic orientations are likely to be stronger in Ukraine
compared to Russia.
Previous studies have also pointed out the political salience of ethnic, language, and
economic factors. These include, for instance, political differences between ethnic Ukrainians
and a Russian minority, and Ukrainian and Russian-speakers (see, for example, Arel, 1995;
Birch, 2000; Bloom and Shulman, 2011; Kuzio, 2011; Wilson, 2005). Many scholars have
frequently described the party system in Ukraine in terms of dichotomous categories of
democratic Orange parties, such as “Our Ukraine” (led by Victor Yushchenko and
Batkivshchyna) “Fatherland” (led by Yulia Tymoshenko), and various undemocratic anti-Orange
parties, such as the Party of Regions (led by Viktor Yanukovych) and the Communist Party (led
by Petro Symonenko). The leaders of these parties (particularly Tymoshenko and Yanukovych)
are presented as polar opposites when it comes to democracy (see, for example, Kuzio, 2011;
Menon and Motyl, 2011).
However, the differences between Orange and anti-Orange parties in regards to
democracy are not as significant as this dichotomy implies. For instance, the Party of Regions
and the Yulia Tymoshenko Bloc, which became the most popular political forces in Ukraine

10

since the “Orange Revolution,” negotiated, prior to the 2010 presidential elections, a secret pact
to form a joint coalition, eliminate direct elections of the president, and divide power between
each other for a long period of time. As noted, the Party of Regions formed a ruling coalition
with the Communist Party, which traces its origins and certain major elements of its ideology to
the totalitarian Communist Party of the Soviet Union. There is certain evidence that the
Yanukovych government bolstered Svoboda. For example, Svoboda leaders received
disproportionally large amounts of time to present their views on several national television
channels that were controlled by the Yanukovych government or government members.
Similarly, Yulia Tymoshenko’s Batkivshchyna, Yushchenko’s “Our Ukraine” party,
Front zmin (Front of Change) led by Arsenii Yatseniuk, and several other parties presenting
themselves as “national democrats” before the 2012 parliamentary elections formed, along with
Svoboda, the Dictatorship Resistance Committee, and most of them agreed to cooperate during
the elections. As noted, this radical nationalist party, which was originally called the Social-
National Party, traces certain key elements of its ideology to the semi-totalitarian fascist
Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists. Both the Party of Regions and Batkivshchyna represent
the interests of oligarchs to a significant extent, and they are motivated less by their public
ideologies than by rent-seeking accomplished with the help of government positions. Both
Yanukovych and Tymoshenko were imprisoned on criminal charges, and there is evidence of
their involvement in large-scale corruption.
Many Western policy-makers, specifically those from America, Britain, Canada,
France, Germany, Sweden, and the European Union, as well as officials from other democratic
countries and international organizations, expressed their support for Yulia Tymoshenko,
declared her conviction as politically motivated, and demanded her release from prison. While

11

Tymoshenko’s conviction in 2011 was partly politically motivated, there are also various pieces
of evidence indicating her involvement in large-scale corruption and her possible involvement in
other types of criminal activity. For example, in 2009 she ordered the signing of a long-term
contract unfavorable to Ukraine for gas supplies from Russia without the required authorization
of the entire government. US prosecutors concluded that Tymoshenko paid bribes to then prime-
minister Pavlo Lazarenko, who was later imprisoned in the US for laundering this money, as a
pay-back for granting her company a monopoly status for gas delivery in Ukraine. Tymoshenko
has been, since the mid-1990s, the target of several criminal investigations involving the bribing
of government officials, foreign currency smuggling, and tax nonpayment in Ukraine and Russia.
She was also investigated in connection with the contract killings of rival businessmen in
Donetsk in the mid-1990s.
While Western policies towards Ukraine, including those in connection with the
Tymoshenko case, are often framed as support for democracy and democratic politicians, these
policies are often driven not by such idealism as democracy promotion, but by realism involving
perceived national interests. For this reason, the policies of the Western states are generally
supportive of semi-democratic or authoritarian governments in pro-Western countries, while
often being opposite concerning other countries with similar levels of democracy. For example,
there are differing US policies concerning such semi-democratic countries as Georgia and
Russia, Kosovo and Abkhazia, and Colombia and Venezuela. The same is true for such
authoritarian states as Azerbaijan and Belarus, Bahrain and Yemen vs. Libya and Syria, Iran or
Iraq under Saddam Hussein vs. Pakistan, and Saudi Arabia vs. Afghanistan under the Taliban.
For the same reason, Western governments have opposed selective arrests or prosecutions for
criminal charges of pro-Western opposition politicians, such as Yulia Tymoshenko and Yurii

12

Lutsenko in post-Orange Ukraine, but not similar selective arrests and criminal charges against
pro-Russian opposition leaders, such as Evhen Kushnaryov and Borys Kolesnikov, after the
“Orange Revolution.”

Methodology and Data
This study uses comparative and statistical analyses of data from the Pew Research
Center’s Global Attitudes surveys, the World Values Surveys, and the IFES surveys to examine
levels and determinants of support for democracy in Ukraine. The paper analyzes the levels of
democratic and authoritarian attitudes in Ukraine compared to Poland and Russia, two
neighboring Slavic post-communist countries, which demonstrated divergent trajectories in terms
of democratization and whose political cultures are linked to different historical and religious
legacies. Poland, which achieved democratic consolidation following the collapse of
communism, is a Catholic country. It experienced a relatively more democratic system compared
to the Russian Empire and the Soviet Union during, respectively, Austro-Hungarian and German
imperial rule in large parts of its regions before World War One and during a significant part of
the period after World War One and before World War Two. In contrast, Russia, which has
experienced a reversal of its democratization since the beginning of the 2000s, is a
predominantly Orthodox country with the relatively more authoritarian legacy of the Russian
Empire and the totalitarian legacy of the Soviet Union.
All surveys analyzed in this study are based on nationally representative weighted
samples.1 The Pew Research Center’s Global Attitudes surveys, the World Values Surveys, and
the IFES surveys provide different measures of democratic attitudes based on different types of
questions concerning various aspects of democracy (see Pew, 2009, 2011; IFES, 2010, 2011;

13

World Values Survey, 2009). This is important, since reliance on a single type of question
concerning democratic orientation can skew the results of an analysis. For example, direct
questions about support for democracy do not necessarily provide exact measures of the level of
democratic values, because respondents often attach different meanings to the term
“democracy.” For instance, in the 2011 IFES survey in Ukraine, largely comparable percentages
of respondents selected such economic statements as “everyone has work” (49%), “state support
of pensioners” (32%), and “state support of those unable to work” (32%), compared to
“protection of human rights” (59%), “freedom of speech” (35%), and “freedom to vote” (25%) to
define meaning of democracy according to their views (IFES, 2011, p. 6).
While responses to direct questions regarding democracy might not be entirely valid as
a measure of the overall levels of democratic values, such questions can be useful for comparing
relative levels of democratic orientation in Ukraine and other countries, such as Poland and
Russia, and their changes over time. The selected surveys also allow examination of changes in
the levels of pro-democratic attitudes in post-Orange Ukraine compared to the Orange and pre-
Orange periods, since attitudes are generally much less stable and much more malleable than
values are. Different waves of the World Values Surveys asked the same questions in each of
these three countries. The same applies to the Pew Global Attitudes surveys, with a certain
variation of one question concerning a multiparty system. However, not all waves of these
surveys were conducted in Poland.
Finally, this study uses multiple regression analysis of the determinants of democratic
attitudes in the 2009 Pew Research Center’s Global Attitudes Survey conducted in Ukraine. The
dependent variables include the following measures of views towards democracy: the democracy
index, support for a change to a multiparty system, relative preference for a democracy vs. a

14

strong leader, and preference for democracy vs. a strong economy. The democracy index is
derived by means of factor analysis based on six questions concerning the importance (for the
respondent) of living in a country where one can openly say what they think and “can criticize
the (state or government),” “honest elections are held regularly with a choice of at least two
political parties,” “there is a judicial system that treats everyone in the same way,” “the military
is under the control of civilian leaders,” “the media can report the news without (state or
government) censorship,” and “you can practice your religion freely.” These questions form a
single factor. The democracy index scale reliability coefficient (alpha) is acceptable (.77). In
addition to standard OLS regressions, a logistic regression analysis is used for analysis of the
determinants of preferences for democracy vs. a strong leader and a strong economy, since these
measures are dummy variables.
Independent variables include such factors such as regional historical experience,
defined by a Western region dummy variable (omitted), religion, political party preference,
ethnicity, language, age, and income. The political party preference dummy variables measure
support for the major political parties that were most popular at the time when the survey was
conducted in 2009, such as the Party of Regions, the Yulia Tymoshenko Bloc, the Our Ukraine-
People’s Self-Defense Bloc, and the Lytvyn Bloc. The Communist Party adherents dummy is the
omitted variable in the regression models, because it is used as a yardstick for comparison of the
effects of other parties. Because of significant increases in support for such parties as Svoboda
and Front zmin have occurred since 2009, the survey did not differentiate adherents of these
political parties. The regression models also control for the effects of such socio-demographic
factors as gender, education level, and place of residence.

Main Results
The 2005-2006 wave of the World Values Surveys demonstrates that the absolute
majority (81%) of the residents of Ukraine, excluding those who were undecided, expressed a
favorable opinion towards a democratic political system soon after the “Orange Revolution.” The
overall level of support for democracy in Ukraine was comparable in the mid-2000s to Poland
(84%) and Russia (79%). However, in the 1995-97 and 1999 waves of this survey, Ukraine was
more supportive of democracy than Russia was. The overall opposition to a democratic system in
Ukraine (20%) was similar to the levels recorded in the mid and late 1990s (20 and 16%,
respectively). The absolute majority of Ukrainian residents, like both their Polish and Russian
counterparts, were opposed to having the army govern their countries. (Table 1).

However, Ukrainians in the mid-2000s were slightly more likely than Russians and
much more likely than Poles were to back a political system with a strong leader who does not
bother with parliament and elections (65, 57, and 31%, respectively). There was a comparable
pattern in the second half of the 1990s (Table 1). The Pew Research Center’s Global Attitudes
Surveys in 2009 demonstrated that respondents from Ukraine were much more supportive of “a
leader with a strong hand” than a democratic form of government, compared to Poles. In
comparison with Russia, the views of Ukrainians on this question were similar in 2011, and less
democratic in 2009 but somewhat more democratic in spring 1991, 2002, and 2007 (see Table 2).
The Pew surveys show that Ukrainians expressed much lower support for a change to a
multiparty system compared to Poles, and, to a somewhat lesser extent, compared to Russians.
For example, in 2009, 36% of the respondents in Ukraine, 61% of those in Russia, and 81% of
those in Poland approved or approved strongly such a change (Table 3). There was a partial

the preferences become very different when a choice is given between a “strong economy” and

The IFES Surveys conducted in post-Orange Ukraine in 2010 and 2011 reveal that the
majority of the respondents did not consider Ukraine a democracy. Similar results on this
question were reported during the “Orange Revolution” (See IFES, 2010, 2011). As in the World
Values Surveys and Pew surveys, the IFES surveys show relative support for a democratic form
of government over a non-democratic one on a direct question, but a different pattern of attitudes
when choices involving trade-offs between the democratic rights of citizens and economic
development or maintenance of order are introduced (Table 5).
The regression analysis of the 2009 Pew Research Center’s Global Attitudes Survey
shows that determinants of democratic attitudes in Ukraine often varied depending upon the
specific measure of these attitudes being used. For example, the average respondent in the South
scored much lower on the democracy index than the average respondent in historically Western
Ukraine, keeping other factors constant. But this regional pattern was reversed in the support for
democracy vs. a strong leader question. The respondents in the East and the Center were much
less likely than their counterparts in the historical West were to express preference for
democracy as opposed to a strong economy. While these effects were statistically significant, the
regional variables were statistically insignificant in all other cases (See Table 6).

Conclusion

18

Although the “Orange Revolution” in Ukraine was generally regarded as a democratic
breakthrough that created favorable conditions for democracy in the nation, this assessment was
quickly reversed after Victor Yanukovych came to power as a result of the 2010 presidential
elections. This study contributes to the analysis of the seemingly shifting prospects for
democracy in Ukraine by focusing on the analysis of democratic and undemocratic values or
political culture, which is an important but understudied factor of democracy.
The analysis of the World Values Surveys, the Pew Global Attitudes surveys, and the
IFES surveys demonstrates that democratic political culture has not become deeply entrenched in
Ukraine. Although the majority of Ukrainians express high levels of support for democracy in
response to direct questions, these seemingly democratic attitudes are, to a significant extent,
shallow. When given an alternative, the majority of respondents express preference for a strong
leader or a strong economy over a democratic system and disapprove of the change to a
multiparty system following the break-up of the Soviet Union.
The basic orientations towards democracy have not changed radically since the
“Orange Revolution” and in post-Orange Ukraine, compared to the pre-Orange era. The most
notable exception is spring 1991, shortly before the collapse of the Soviet communism and
independence of Ukraine, but such anomalous attitudes can be attributed to the support of
various forms of democratization at that time by both the nationalist opposition and the reformist
Communist Party leadership led by Mikhail Gorbachev. Comparative analysis shows that
respondents in Ukraine are closer to Russia than to Poland on many measures of democratic
attitudes, and their values are generally much less democratic compared to those held in Poland.
There are regional differences in attitudes towards certain dimensions of democracy.
These can be attributed to differences in political culture due to distinct historical legacies in

19

Western Ukraine compared to other parts of Ukraine. However, statistical analysis offers only
partial empirical support for the notion of a relatively more democratic culture in Western
Ukraine. The same applies to the relatively more democratic attitudes of Catholics compared to
Orthodox Christians in Ukraine. These differences, which are also limited to certain measures of
democratic orientations, can be linked to distinct religious legacies.
While Ukrainian politics are often presented as being primarily characterized by the
contrasts between democratic Orange parties and leaders and their undemocratic anti-Orange
counterparts, the analysis of the 2009 Pew survey data shows that supporters of major parties on
both sides of the political spectrum, in particular, the Yulia Tymoshenko Bloc and the Party of
Regions, do not differ significantly in terms of their attitudes towards democracy compared to
those of the Communist Party. The Our Ukraine-People’s Self-Defense Bloc is the partial
exception to this pattern, but its public support had largely collapsed by the end of the Orange
period when its leader, Viktor Yushchenko, was President of Ukraine.
This study suggests ceteris paribus that the prospects for democracy in Ukraine are
impeded by partially democratic and partially authoritarian values. This feature of the nation’s
political culture makes the future of Ukraine more compatible with a semi-democratic hybrid
political system, such as that which emerged in such countries as post-Soviet Russia, than with a
consolidated democracy, such as the type that developed in post-communist Poland. However,
although political culture evolves gradually over generations, it is only one among a number of
important factors of democracy. Other factors, which can change over a relatively short period of
time (for example, the EU offering Ukraine a real prospect for membership), can still make
consolidated democracy in Ukraine possible, even though this is not very likely to happen.
