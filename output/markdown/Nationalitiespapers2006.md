Regional Political Divisions in Ukraine in 1991–2006/C3
Ivan Katchanovski
Introduction
This article examines determinants of persistent regional political cleavages in post-
Communist Ukraine. The question is how signiﬁcant the role of culture is compared
to ethnic, economic, and religious factors in the regional divisions. This study employs
correlation, factor, and regression analyses of regional support for the Communist/
pro-Russian parties and presidential candidates and pro-nationalist/pro-independence
parties and candidates in all national elections held from 1991 to 2006, the vote for
the preservation of the Soviet Union in the March 1991 referendum, and the vote
for the independence of Ukraine in the December 1991 referendum. This study
shows that the pattern of these regional differences remained relatively stable from
1991 to 2006. Historical experience has a major effect on regional electoral behavior
in post-Communist Ukraine. The legacy of Austro-Hungarian, Polish, Romanian,
and Czechoslovak rule is positively associated with the pro-nationalist and pro-
independence vote; the same historical legacy has a negative effect on support for
pro-Communist and pro-Russian parties and presidential candidates and on the vote
for the preservation of the Soviet Union.
Research Question
Strong regional political cleavages manifested themselves in Ukraine after the
collapse of Communism. In elections and referendums held from 1991 to 2006,
Western Ukrainian regions have generally voted for pro-nationalist parties and poli-
ticians and favored the independence of Ukraine from the Soviet Union. In contrast,
many Eastern regions have often backed pro-Communist and pro-Russian parties
and politicians and supported the preservation of the Soviet Union.
For instance, ofﬁcial results of the repeat second round of the 2004 presidential
elections during the “Orange Revolution” showed that Viktor Yushchenko, a pro-
Western candidate supported by nationalist parties, received more than 90% of
votes in four Western Ukrainian regions, compared to less than 25% in four Eastern
Ukraine regions. Conversely, Viktor Yanukovych, a pro-Russian candidate, received
more than 70% of the votes in the four regions in Eastern Ukraine and less than 7% of

The question is whether the pro-nationalist/pro-independence and pro-Communist/
pro-Russian voting behavior in regions of Ukraine is stable across the various
elections and referendums held in the period 1991–2006 and whether this pattern
of regional political orientation can be attributed primarily to the regional political
culture or to other factors.
This article quantitatively assesses the role of political culture compared to
economic, ethnic, religious, and demographic factors on pro-Russian/pro-Communist
orientation and nationalist/pro-independence orientation that manifested themselves
in the regional results of all national presidential and parliamentary elections in
Ukraine during the period 1991–2006, as well as in two referendums held in 1991.
This study uses historical experience as a measure of regional political culture. In
contrast to most previous studies, this article uses the pre-1939 Soviet border to
deﬁne the Western and Eastern regions of Ukraine. The study examines the predictive
power of the regional historical experience variable, the Western historical region
variable, and the religious variable, as compared to other regional variables that
have been suggested by previous studies.

Previous studies have identiﬁed different factors as responsible for regional pol-

5
Ethnic theories, which link regional cleavages to differences and rivalries
between ethnic or linguistic minorities and majorities, focus on the presence of
the large number of ethnic Russians and Russian speakers in the Eastern part of
Ukraine.
6 Some studies, which rely on theories of relative deprivation and uneven
economic development, link the regional divisions in Ukraine to the differences
in levels of regional economic development, urbanization, and industrialization.
These studies attribute regional cleavages to higher levels of economic develop-
ment, urbanization, and industrialization in most regions of Eastern Ukraine relative
to Western Ukraine.
7
A growing number of studies link the regional cleavages in Ukraine to differences
in political culture, in particular to distinct historical and religious legacies. Ukraine
consists of regions whose historical development and political systems differed
signiﬁcantly before World War II than after. It was only by the end of the war that
all of the regions that are now part of independent Ukraine became uniﬁed under
Soviet rule.8
In addition, several Western Ukrainian regions are mostly Greek Catholic, in
contrast to the predominantly Orthodox regions of Eastern Ukraine. These religious
differences contributed in the past to the evolution of differing political cultures in
regions of Ukraine. The divisions among the faithful of different religions, primarily
the division between Orthodox Christians and Greek Catholics, are sometimes
identiﬁed as a separate factor in regional cleavages. Religious conﬂicts resurfaced
after the ban on the Greek Catholic Church was lifted in the Soviet Union in 1989;
this was shortly before Ukraine became independent.
9
Previous studies of regional political divisions in Ukraine typically have analyzed
the results of one or two elections or surveys, and the studies disagree in their inter-
pretation of regional differences.10 These studies employ different classiﬁcations of
## regions in Ukraine. Many scholars divide Ukraine into four geographical regions
West, Center, South, and East. Other scholars divide Ukraine into Western and
Eastern parts along the Dnieper (Dnipro) River. Such classiﬁcations are based to a
signiﬁcant extent on geographical location, and they are affected by subjective judg-
ment.

Political Culture in Regions of Ukraine
The term “regional political culture” refers to values and norms that are shared by
many residents of a particular region. Most theories of political culture maintain
that political values change slowly over a span of generations. Political culture is
not the same as ideology; however, over a long period of time, political values are
often expressed, for example, in greater support for liberal, conservative, or nationalist
political orientation in one country or region than in another country or a region.
12
Political culture theories imply a relatively stable pattern of basic political values or
general political orientations over a long period of time. In contrast to values, political
attitudes, which manifest themselves in a support of a particular political party or a
particular political leader in a particular election, are much more volatile. Not all indi-
viduals in a particular region share the political culture of that region. People may vote
out of self-interest, for economic reasons, because of the charisma of a politician, or
because of other factors. The emergence of new issues sometimes leads to an electoral
realignment in regional support for certain parties and politicians.13
Historical experience or legacy represents a major source of political culture.14
Shared historical experiences shape the values and norms of individuals in the same
direction, while different histories the opposite effect. Socialization within family,
school, church, and circles of friends serves as a mechanism to transmit these
values and norms from one generation to another. Critical junctures of history, such
as the uniﬁcation or break-up of a country, provide a major impetus for the formation
of political culture. A critical juncture represents the point in the evolution of a pol-
itical culture when the dice become “loaded.”
15 Subsequent historical experience
reinforces this bias in patterns of orientations.
Voter alignment and party systems in many advanced Western countries reﬂect
historical cleavage structures. Different historical experiences in the North and the
South of Italy account for variations in the institutional performance of regional
governments. Because of differences in colonial institutions, the legacy of British
rule in the United States and Canada was more favorable, compared with the legacy
of Spanish and Portuguese rule in Latin America, to the emergence and evolution
of decentralized government in post-colonial times. Historical legacies and religious
traditions have led to divergence in the levels of corruption, economic liberalization
and economic growth in post-Communist countries.
16
Ukraine includes regions that have belonged to different states for signiﬁcant
historical periods of time. The Ivano-Frankivsk Region, the Lviv Region, and most
of the Ternopil Region were part of Galicia province in the Austro-Hungarian
Monarchy from 1772 to World War I. These regions then became part of Poland
until the Soviet–Nazi pact of 1939. Similarly, the Chernivtsi Region (Bukovyna),
with the exception of its eastern portion, was ruled by the Hapsburg Empire from
1774 to World War I, and then by Romania until World War II. Transcarpathia
belonged to the Hungarian part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire until World War I

and then to Czechoslovakia until World War II. The Volyn Region and the Rivne
Region, both parts of Volhynia province, belonged to Poland in the period between
the two World Wars. All these regions, which collectively are often referred to in
Ukraine as “Western Ukraine,” were incorporated into the Soviet Union as a result
of the Soviet–Nazi pact of 1939 and World War II.
In contrast, regions of historical Eastern Ukraine, that is, all regions located to the
East of Western Ukraine, were ruled by the Russian Empire from the end of the eight-
eenth century and then by the Soviet Union. Interruptions of Russian and Soviet rule in
Eastern Ukraine were brief. These interruptions were caused mainly by warfare, such
as the several-year-long Nazi occupation during World War II.
The division of Ukraine, ﬁrst between the Russian Empire and the Austro-
Hungarian Empire in the late eighteenth century and then between the Soviet Union
and Poland, Romania, and Czechoslovakia in the early twentieth century, were
crucial junctures in the emergence and evolution of distinct regional political cultures.
These territorial divisions coincided with an important period in the formation of
national identity. Before nationalism had begun to spread in the late eighteenth and
early nineteenth centuries, local, religious, and status-based identities were dominant
among various strata of the population in Ukraine.
17
Historical conditions were generally more favorable for the spread of nationalism
and the development of a national identity in the Austro-Hungarian Monarchy and
in interwar Czechoslovakia, Poland, and Romania than in the Ukrainian parts of the
Russian Empire and the Soviet Union. A relatively more democratic political
system in the Austro-Hungarian Empire, compared to that in the Russian Empire,
gave Ukrainian intellectuals more opportunity to spread their ideas and cultivate a
national identity. Language, education, and religious policies in Western Ukrainian
regions, in particular in the Austro-Hungarian province of Galicia, were also more
favorable to the development of Ukrainian national identity.18 The Ukrainian Greek
Catholic Church in Galicia played a signiﬁcant role in the development of a Ukrainian
national identity, in contrast to the Orthodox Church in Ukrainian regions of the
Russian Empire.19
In contrast to the Austro-Hungarian Empire, the Russian government eliminated
Ukrainian regional autonomy, prohibited the activity of Ukrainian political organiz-
ations and associations, and restricted the use of the Ukrainian language in education
and print. The Tsar’s government fostered a Russian imperial identity, and treated
Ukrainians as a regional group and the Ukrainian language as a dialect of Russian.
Because of differences in political systems, Ukrainians in the regions under Soviet
rule had less freedom of political association, electoral politics, and national cultural
development than did Ukrainians in regions under Czechoslovak, Polish, and
Romanian rule in the interwar period.
20
The historical differences between Western and Eastern Ukrainian regions do not
imply that all regions in each of these two groups have homogenous political cultures.

Data and Methodology
The pro-Communist/pro-Russian vote and the nationalist/pro-independence vote in
Ukraine are the dependent variables. Previous studies, Ukrainian and Western
media publications, and ﬁeld research in Ukraine during various periods from 1991
to 2006 are used to identify political parties, electoral blocs, and presidential candi-
dates that most closely match these political orientations. Only parties and presidential
candidates that emphasize their pro-Communist /pro-Russian or nationalist /pro-
independence orientations are included in the statistical analysis, while parties and
leaders mostly associated with other political orientations (e.g. oligarchic and
liberal) are excluded.
Data on the pro-Communist /pro-Russian vote and the pro-nationalist /pro-
independence vote in regions of Ukraine are derived from the results of presidential
and parliamentary elections and referendums on the preservation of the Soviet
Union and Ukrainian independence that were held between 1991 and 2006
(Table 1). The 1996 World Values Survey (WVS) in Ukraine provides individual

Correlation and factor analysis of the national election and referendum results
from 1991 to 2006 in 26 Ukrainian regions shows a remarkable persistence of the
regional cleavages. The combined vote for the nationalist/pro-independence pres-
idential candidates and parties in each presidential and parliamentary election is
highly intercorrelated. The correlations range from 0.51 to 0.97. The vote for the Com-
munist and pro-Russian parties and presidential candidates in Ukrainian regions also
shows a highly consistent pattern. The correlations range from 0.47 to 0.997. All these
correlations are statistically signiﬁcant; the overwhelming majority is signiﬁcant at the
0.001 level. In other words, the probability they arose by chance in most cases is equal
to or less than 1 in 1000, and in the rest of the cases is equal to or less than 1 in 100 or 1
in 20 (see Tables 3 and 4).
It is noteworthy that regional support for the pro-nationalist/pro-independence
parties and presidential candidates and the pro-Communist /pro-Russian parties
and candidates since the “Orange Revolution” is strongly correlated with regional
support for electoral forces of the same political orientation before the “Orange
Revolution.” The correlation coefﬁcients for the combined support for the “Our
Ukraine” Bloc, the Ukrainian People’s Bloc, and several smaller parties in the
2006 elections range from 0.81 (the vote for Viktor Yushchenko in the repeat
second round of the 2004 presidential elections) to 0.97 (the vote for the “Our
Ukraine” Bloc in the 2002 parliamentary elections). The regional vote for Viktor
Yushchenko in the repeat second round of the 2004 presidential elections has the
weakest correlation (0.51) with support for Leonid Kuchma in the second round
of the 1999 presidential elections, and the strongest correlation (0.83) with the
vote for the “Our Ukraine” Bloc in the 2002 parliamentary elections. The combined
vote for the pro-Russian and pro-Communist parties in 2006 has the weakest corre-
lation (0.49) with the regional vote for Leonid Kravchuk in the 1991 presidential
elections, and the strongest correlation (0.997) with the vote for Viktor Yanukovych
in the repeat second round of the 2004 presidential elections. Regional support for
Viktor Yanukovych in the 2004 presidential election correlates most weakly
(0.47) with the combined vote for pro-Communist and pro-Russian parties in the
2002 elections (Tables 3 and 4).

Regression Analysis
Multiple regression analysis shows that historical experience has the strongest effect
on the regional pro-Communist and pro-Russian vote index from 1991 to 2006. This is
the case in all versions of regression models. These regression models differ by type of
historical experience variable and by inclusion of either the proportion of the
Ukrainian speakers or the proportion of ethnic Ukrainians, because these two variables
are extremely highly intercorrelated (0.97) and cannot be included in the same
regression for the reason of multicollinearity.
The historical variable, expressed as the number of years of non-Russian/non-
Soviet rule, has the largest standardized (beta) coefﬁcient, and is statistically sign-
iﬁcant at p /C200.001 level at a regression model I, which includes the proportion of
Ukrainian speakers, and atp /C200.01 level at the another version of the same model,
which includes the proportion of ethnic Ukrainians. Keeping other variables constant,
10 years of non-Russian/non-Soviet rule decrease the pro-Communist/pro-Russian
vote index by 1.7%. The use of the Western historical regions dummy variable
instead of the years of non-Russian/non-Soviet rule shows similar results. The
Western historical regions variable is the strongest predictor of the pro-Communist/
pro-Russian vote index. This measure of the regional historical experience is statisti-
cally signiﬁcant at thep /C200.001 level. When other factors, such as the urbanization
rate, the proportion of Catholics, the proportion of ethnic Ukrainians, or the proportion
of native Ukrainian speakers are kept constant, the average Western region scores
about 22% lower than the average historical Eastern region on the pro-Communist/
pro-Russian regional vote index (Table 5).

based on the years of non-Russian /non-Soviet rule and the Western historical

Ukraine, which as an omitted variable in the regressions serves as a yardstick for

the 0.001 level. The differences between historical Western Ukraine and each of

Conclusion
Post-Communist Ukraine has experienced signiﬁcant regional cleavages since 1991.
Statistical analysis of the regional voting results in all national parliamentary and
presidential elections and two key referendums from 1991 to 2006 shows a stability
of pro-nationalist/pro-independence orientation and pro-Communist /pro-Russian
orientation in regions of Ukraine in spite of changes in dominant political parties
and political leaders. Regional support for nationalist /pro-independence and
pro-Communist/pro-Russian parties and presidential candidates since the “Orange
Revolution” is strongly correlated with regional support for parties and candidates
of the similar political orientation before the “Orange Revolution.”
