T
h i s study analyzes the coverage of post-communist
countries, specifically their politics, by the main
television networks in the United states. It compares the
extent and the content of the reporting on post-communist
states such as Russia, Ukraine, Poland, Romania, Bul-
garia, the czech Republic, hungary, Belarus, Kazakhstan,
Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, Georgia, turkmenistan, tajiki-
stan, armenia, azerbaijan, and Moldova. the question is
whether political factors, such as relations with the United
states, affect american television coverage of these
countries. a related research question is whether there
are significant differences in the extent and the content of
coverage of former communist states on major television
news programs in the United states.
analyzing the coverage of post-communist nations
by U.s. television networks is important not only from
a political communications perspective but also from a
comparative politics and foreign policy perspective. Previ-
ous studies show that media coverage affects the attitudes
of the american public and the United states’ foreign
policy agenda toward other countries—in particular,
Russia (Brewer 2006; norris 2000; semetko et al. 1992;
Wanta et al. 2004). Western mass-media reporting about
post-communist countries became an important political
concern in many of these states. In addition, a growing
number of politicians and journalists from post-commu-
nist nations, specifically from Russia and Poland, raise
the question of political biases in the coverage of these
countries by mass communications media in the United
states. For example, the inosMI.ru Web site, established
by the state-owned RIa novosti, specializes in publishing
online translations of stories from Western newspapers
and magazines concerning Russia. In 2005, the Russian

the first hypothesis tested in this paper is that U.s.
allies among post-communist countries are likely to re-
ceive much better coverage compared to adversaries and
non-allies, not only in the areas of relations between these
countries and the United states but also on other issues,
including nonpolitical concerns. this relationship is de-
rived from the indexing theory of political communication
and a theory of american political culture. the indexing
hypothesis implies that media coverage of U.s. foreign
policy mostly reflects the framing and political agendas
of government officials. this mechanism was manifested
by studying the framing of the Iraq War in U.s. media.
Prior to and during the Iraq War, the mainstream media
mostly followed the Bush administration and reported
their concerns regarding Iraqi weapons of mass destruc-
tion (WMD) and the Iraqi government’s association with
al Qaeda despite a lack of sufficient evidence (Bennett
1990; Bennett et al. 2007; hayes and Guardino 2010;
Mermin 1999).
our study argues that indexing affects the coverage
of U.s. relations with post-communist states as well as
of issues such as domestic politics, sports, crime, the
economy, and immigrants. Because of the strong moral-
istic elements in american political culture, allies of the
United states are often idealized and represented as the
embodiment of “good,” while adversaries are presented as
“evil.” For example, Joseph stalin was turned into “Uncle
Joe” when the soviet Union and the United states were
allies during World War II, and his totalitarian rule and
mass political repression were largely overlooked in the
United states until the start of the cold war, when stalin
and the soviet Union were presented in a negative light
by U.s. officials and the media (levering 1976; lipset
1996, p. 66). similarly, afghan mujahideen were often
represented in the U.s. media as “freedom fighters” during
their war in afghanistan against the soviet Union, despite
their illiberal Islamist ideology—reflected in their name,
which is derived from jihad.
U.s. allies are defined in this study as members of the
north atlantic treaty organization (nato) or countries
that pursue pro-Western policies, including the intention
to join nato. Poland, the czech Republic, and hun-
gary joined nato at the beginning of 1999; Romania,
Bulgaria, and slovakia were granted membership in this
military and political alliance in 2004. With the exception
of the Baltic states, post-soviet countries did not become
nato members. the governments of Georgia after the
Rose Revolution at the end of 2003 and Ukraine after the
orange Revolution in late 2004, however, adopted a pro-
Western orientation in their foreign policy and declared
nato membership a priority. although nato did not
offer membership to these states, in april 2008 it did
recognize Georgia and Ukraine as potential members,
with U.s. government support.
In addition, certain non-nato post-communist states
allied with the United states, to one degree or another,
during the wars in Kosovo, Iraq, and afghanistan. the
war in Kosovo in 1999 took place under nato auspices,
and the U. s. military presence in afghanistan came
under a formal nato umbrella in 2003. Because these
tactical alliances were limited to the conflicts in Kosovo,
Iraq, and afghanistan—and were much shorter in dura-
tion and much less formal, comprehensive, and stable
than nato—their effect on U. s. television coverage
of particular post-communist states is likely to be less
pronounced but still significant. We can expect to see
evidence of this effect in the news coverage of respec-
tive countries’ roles in these conflicts and changes in the
coverage of post-communist countries before and after
these conflicts.
our second hypothesis is that coverage of Russia by
U.s. television networks is negative. this hypothesis is
derived from indexing theory, other theories of media
bias, and previous studies. the political elite in the
United states has been historically biased against Rus-
sia, in contrast to many other states that were or still are
communist, such as china (Davis and trani 2009; lieven
2000a; tsygankov 2009). this bias is likely to manifest
itself in television coverage of post-communist Russia.
Most previous studies have found a negative pattern of
the coverage of this post-communist country.
our third hypothesis is that coverage of other post-
soviet countries is likely to be affected by the anti-Russian
bias. the U.s. media often referred to the soviet Union
as Russia and may still to a significant extent associate
many post-soviet countries with Russia.
although bias can be difficult to determine precisely,
and the concept of bias is often dismissed as too sub-
jective, many studies address various media biases in
political communications and related fields. Media bias
is often defined as unbalanced coverage in terms of the
amount of positive and negative content. D’alessio and
## allen (2000) define the following types of media bias
gatekeeping bias, which involves the inclusion or exclu-
sion of certain stories; coverage bias, which relates to the
relative extent of coverage; and statement bias, which
deals with favorability of coverage.

Media bias is also often defined in terms of the politi-
cal preferences of journalists or their audiences. some
studies argue that the U.s. media as a whole has a liberal
bias, because journalists are more politically liberal than
the general public (Groseclose 2011). other studies
maintain that significant differences exist in this regard
among U.s. media. Whereas certain television or cable
networks—aBc, nBc, cBs, PBs, and cnn—are re-
garded as relatively liberal, others, such as Fox news, are
relatively conservative. similarly, while public radio sta-
tions like nPR are perceived to be more liberal, talk radio
is perceived as more conservative. there is an analogous
dichotomy between more liberal newspapers, such as
the New York Times and the Washington Post, and more
conservative counterparts such as the Wall Street Journal
and the Washington Times (Jamieson and cappella 2008;
Iyengar and hahn 2009; Morris 2007).
Media bias is also defined as deviation from reality or
facts. this type of bias is exemplified by the misrepre-
sentation in many U.s. media reports of Iraq as having
WMD and the Iraq government under saddam hussein
as having ties to al Qaeda before the onset of the U.s.-led
war with this Middle eastern country in 2003 (Bennett
et al. 2007; hayes and Guardino 2010; Kuypers 2006).
another instance is the portrayal of Russia as an aggressor
during the Georgian–Russian war by many U.s. media
outlets, even though Georgia launched the war when it
attacked its de facto independent breakaway province of
south ossetia and the Russian peacekeepers stationed
there (Bahador and Katchanovski 2010; heinrich and
tanaev 2009).
Previous Studies
the present article is one of the first academic studies
to examine the reporting on post-communist countries
by U.s. television networks. Most previous studies have
focused on the representation of a single country—Russia,
Poland, Ukraine, or Romania—in the Western mass media
(Golan and Wanta 2003; hickman and trapp 1998; Media
tenor 2004, 2007a, 2007b; norris 1995; Russia 2004;
semetko et al. 1992). Many studies analyzed coverage
of these states only in major print media sources in the
United states and other Western countries (Bohush 2009;
hickman and trapp 1998). U.s. television networks, how-
ever, reach a much greater audience than newspapers and
magazines (norris 2000; Pew Research center 2008).
the absolute majority of previous studies deal with
various types of media biases concerning domestic issues
in the United states. For example, previous studies have
examined various types of U.s. media bias in coverage of
the Democratic and Republican presidential candidates,
female presidential contenders, and various racial, ethnic,
and religious minorities (D’alessio and allen 2000; Falk
2010). empirical analyses of U.s. television news have
also reported “home bias” in the amount of coverage
given to domestic stories compared to stories dealing with
foreign countries (Pew Research center 1995). studies
of bias in U.s. coverage of foreign countries since sep-
tember 11 focus on the Iraq War and other conflicts in the
Middle east or the representation of Muslims and arabs
by the mass media in the United states and other Western
countries (Bennett et al. 2007; hayes and Guardino 2010;
Kuypers 2006; Poole and Richardson 2006).
content analysis in previous studies has shown that
Russia receives significantly more coverage in U.s. me-
dia than other post-communist countries do. a sample
of evening news programs on aBc, cBs, and nBc in
1988–92 revealed that 17.3 percent of all foreign news
stories were devoted to the soviet Union or Russia, com-
pared to 2.3 percent to Poland, 2.0 percent to czechoslo-
vakia, and 1.2 percent to yugoslavia (hess 1996, p. 32).
In January–october 1998, aBc, cBs, nBc, and cnn
news broadcasts produced 342 stories on Russia, 64 on
Bosnia, 18 on the Baltic states, and 12 on Poland (Wanta
et al. 2004, p. 371).
Both of these studies were conducted in the 1990s.
they reveal that Russia and the soviet Union before its
collapse in 1991 received the most foreign coverage from
the main U.s. television networks. Russia’s prominence in
the U.s. media declined significantly after september 11
because of the shifting focus toward Middle eastern coun-
tries, especially Iraq and afghanistan. content analysis of
evening news programs by aBc, cBs, and nBc, Time,
and Newsweek between July 2003 and July 2004 showed
that Russia dropped to the second dozen most covered
countries in U.s. media. With regard to post-communist
states, however, Russia remained the leader in terms
of U. s. news media attention. a much higher number
(116) of news reports dealt with Russia than with other
post-communist countries (61). among post-soviet and
east central european countries, Georgia, Bulgaria, and
Poland were the subject of the largest number of stories
(Media tenor 2004).

content analysis of television news programs on aBc,

some academic studies indicate that U.s. news media
frequently equate Russia with the soviet Union, and this
misrepresentation extends to post-soviet states. For ex-
ample, american mass media often refer to members of
organized crime from post-soviet countries as “Russian
mafia,” even though academic studies reveal that among
immigrants from the former soviet Union in the United
states, most criminals are not Russian citizens or ethni-
cally Russian. similarly, organized crime networks in
Russia include a large proportion of ethnic minorities, pri-
marily chechens, and people from post-soviet countries
of the caucasus region, especially Georgia (Finckenauer
and Waring 1998).
commercial firms have conducted a few studies of the
representation of Ukraine by Western mass media, but
they have not released all their findings (Bohush 2009;
Media tenor 2007b). the Western mass media usually
associate Ukraine with the orange Revolution, political
instability, the chernobyl nuclear disaster, and conten-
tious relations with Russia (Bohush 2009). U.s. media
coverage of Ukraine-related companies—as is also true of
reporting on Poland, croatia, and many other east central
european and Balkan countries—was generally positive
in the first half of 2007. this contrasted with mostly nega-
tive american media coverage of Russian and Belarusian
companies. With the exception of Russia, however, the
number of reports on Ukraine and other post-communist
states in that study was extremely small, typically several
per country (Media tenor 2007b).
the “color revolutions” in Georgia in 2003, Ukraine in
2004, and Kyrgyzstan in 2005 received significant media
attention in the West. Previous studies argue that Western
reporting concerning these political developments has
been overwhelmingly positive and similar to coverage
of the velvet Revolution in czechoslovakia in 1989.
Western media presented mass political demonstrations
in response to attempts at election fraud as democratic
pro-Western revolutions (schipani-adúriz 2007).

Main Results
the analysis of television news programs on aBc, cBs,
and nBc from 1998 to 2009 indicates a bias in terms
of the amount of coverage of various post-communist
countries when the size of their population is taken into
account. It shows that Russia received disproportionate
coverage compared to other post-soviet countries. the
number and length of stories dealing with Russia and
Russians were six and five times higher, respectively,
than the other eleven post- soviet countries combined,
even though their population size is similar. this dispar-
ity, however, has declined significantly since 2004. the
number of Russia-related stories, compared to the other
post-soviet states, was 3.5 times higher in 2004–9 and
8.5 times higher in 1998–2003. the difference in the
length of reports diminished from six to four times. the
relatively large number of stories dealing with Georgia is
mostly due to its august 2008 war with Russia. Belarus,
turkmenistan, and Moldova received very little coverage
in U.s. television news. (see table 1.)
the post-soviet states, excluding Russia, also received
less coverage (four-fifths) than their east central euro-
pean counterparts, even though the combined population
of the post-soviet countries was about 1.5 times greater.
the disparity in the extent of reporting has not changed
since 2004, although the disparity in the number of stories
declined somewhat (t able 1). a similar pattern char -
acterized most individual post-soviet and east central
european countries with a similar population size. For
example, Ukraine, Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan, and Belarus
received much less television coverage in 1998–2009 than
their east central european counterparts with comparable
populations. Ukraine lagged significantly behind Poland
on both measures of coverage. the number of television
reports and the length of the stories concerning Uzbekistan
and Kazakhstan were considerably smaller than those of
Romania. similarly, Belarus lagged significantly behind
hungary, Bulgaria, and the czech Republic. coverage of
Georgia, armenia, and Kyrgyzstan, however, was more

Conclusion
our analysis of major aBc, cBs, and nBc news pro-
grams in 1998–2009 shows significant differences in the
quantity and content of U.s. television coverage of post-
communist countries. Post-soviet states such as Ukraine,
Kazakhstan, azerbaijan, and Belarus, which became
independent only in 1991, received disproportionately
low coverage on U. s. television compared to Russia
and countries in east central europe relative to their
population size. this study indicates that one of the main
factors that accounts for such disparity is the continuing
association of the former soviet republics with Russia in
many reports. the length of broadcasts that directly or
indirectly misidentified Russia with the soviet Union and
post-soviet countries was comparable to all reportage on
the other post-soviet states.
the television coverage of U.s. allies among post-
communist countries—Poland, hungary, and the czech
Republic—was much more favorable than that on U.s.
adversaries and non-allies (Belarus, Russia, Uzbekistan,
and Kazakhstan). american television broadcasts con-
cerning Bulgaria, slovakia, Ukraine, and Georgia became
significantly more favorable after these countries joined
nato or adopted pro-U.s. foreign policies. the effect
of tactical alliances during the wars in Kosovo, Iraq, and
afghanistan on the coverage of post-communist nations
was less pronounced but still significant in a number of
cases.
